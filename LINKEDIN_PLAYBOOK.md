# The LinkedIn Headline & About Playbook

A no-BS guide to writing a LinkedIn **headline** and **About** section that gets you
*found* by recruiters and *remembered* by humans — and the exact prompts to make any AI
tool (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) write them with you.

Steal it. Share it. Adapt it to your field.

---

## TL;DR

**Headline formula:**
`[Exact Role Title(s)] | [punchy line — the problem you solve] | [keyword stack] | [credential]`

**About structure (pick one):**
- **Frame A — Template:** Intro (who + value) → 3 proof bullets (with numbers) → Close (what you want + contact)
- **Frame B — Conversion copy:** Pain hook → turning-point story → quantified outcomes → who you're for → one CTA

**Experience entries:** role summary line (light first person) → 3–6 impact bullets (implied first person) → per-role skill tags → media. Don't paste resume bullets verbatim.

**Featured:** 3–6 curated cards, portfolio first. Write your own keyword-rich title + hook on each; use clean thumbnails.

**Photo & banner:** clear head-and-shoulders photo; a custom banner (1584x396) that represents you — tagline, stack, or portfolio URL.

**The two rules under everything:**
1. **Search:** use the *exact words* recruiters type. They search phrases, not vibes.
2. **Human:** lead with the problem you solve, not your job title. Numbers build trust.

---

## Why this matters (10 seconds)

Your headline and About do two jobs at once:
- **Get found** — LinkedIn search indexes both. The right keywords surface you.
- **Get chosen** — once found, a human decides in seconds. Story + proof wins.

Most profiles do one or neither. Do both and you're already ahead.

# Part 1 — The Headline

Your headline is your single most important search field. It shows up everywhere your
name does — search results, comments, connection requests. ~220 characters. Make each one count.

## The formula

```
[Exact Role Title(s)] | [punchy purpose line] | [keyword stack] | [credential / school]
```

### Why each block works

| Block | What it does | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Exact role title(s)** | "Product Designer & UX Researcher" | Recruiters search *phrases*. They type "Product Designer" — LinkedIn looks for those two words *adjacent*. Write "Design & Research Pro" and you match **neither** search. Spell the title out. |
| **Punchy purpose line** | The problem you solve, in plain words | Titles say what you *are*. This says what you *do* — the human hook that makes someone stop and read. |
| **Keyword stack** | Tools, skills, specialties | Each term is a search you can win. This is where you get *found* for specifics. |
| **Credential** | School, certification, or standout marker | Cheap credibility + extra searchable terms. |

### Worked example (fictional persona: **Maya, a UX designer**)

> **Weak (what most people write):**
> `Creative problem-solver passionate about beautiful experiences ✨`
> — No role title to match. No keywords. No proof. Invisible to search, forgettable to humans.

> **Strong:**
> `Product Designer & UX Researcher | I turn confusing products into ones people actually finish using | Figma · Design Systems · Usability Testing · Accessibility | ex-Fintech`

Notice: exact titles up front (matches "Product Designer" *and* "UX Researcher"
searches), a purpose line that names a real problem, a keyword stack of searchable
skills, and a quick credibility marker.

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Spell out the full role title(s) recruiters actually search.
- Front-load the searchable terms (don't bury them after a long phrase).
- Make the purpose line about *their* problem, not your adjectives.
- Use `·` or `|` to separate keywords cleanly.

**Don't**
- Hide your title behind clever joins ("Design & Strategy Lead" breaks "Designer" search).
- Keyword-stuff with zero voice — reads like spam, repels humans.
- Use only soft words ("passionate," "creative," "driven") — they're unsearchable and say nothing.
- Blow past ~220 characters — the tail gets cut off.

## Prompt: generate your headline

```
You are a LinkedIn optimization expert. Help me write my headline.

First, interview me — ask 3-4 short questions about:
- the exact role title(s) I want recruiters to find me under
- the #1 problem I solve for the teams/clients I serve
- my personality and how I want to come across (bold, grounded, builder, etc.)
- my top searchable skills/tools

Then give me 5 headline options using this formula:
[Exact Role Title(s)] | [punchy line about the problem I solve] | [keyword stack] | [credential]

Rules:
- Spell out exact role titles so recruiter phrase-search matches them.
- The purpose line should be human and specific, not generic adjectives.
- Stay under 220 characters.
- Tell me which one you recommend and why.

Here's my background: [paste your resume or a few bullet points].
```

# Part 2 — The About

The About section is where a curious recruiter decides if you're worth a message.
It's also **indexed for search**, so it carries keywords too. Limit: ~2,600 characters.
Sweet spot: ~2,000. First 2-3 lines show before the "see more" cut — earn the click.

Two frames. Pick based on how bold you want to be.

## Frame A — The Template (clean, safe, fast)

Best when you want something solid quickly, or you're in a conservative field.

```
[Action verb] [your role] with [X] years across [field]. I help [who] do [value + proof].

Some things I've done:
• [Win #1 — with a number]
• [Win #2 — with a number]
• [Win #3 — with a number]

I'm looking for [the specific kind of role/work you want].

Reach me at [email].
```

**Why it works:** clear, skimmable, proof-driven. The numbered wins do the persuading.

## Frame B — Conversion Copy (memorable, stands out)

Best when you want to be *remembered*, not just listed. Reads like a story, not a résumé.

```
[A pain your reader feels — one punchy line.]

[The twist: and here's what usually goes wrong.]

I'm [name]. I [what you do that fixes it].

[A turning-point moment that taught you your point of view.]

What that looks like in practice:
• [Win #1 — what you built ; the result, with a number]
• [Win #2 — same shape]
• [Win #3 — same shape]

I do my best work with [the kind of team/client you fit].

[One clear call to action + contact.]
```

## The patterns that make About sections convert

Each pattern: **what it is → why it works → how to apply.**

1. **Hook = your reader's pain, not your title**
   *Why:* Stops the scroll. Signals you understand *their* problem before you pitch yourself.
   *How:* Open on the frustration your audience feels. ("Most onboarding flows lose users on day one.")

2. **A turning-point story**
   *Why:* A moment is memorable; a skills list isn't. It shows you earned a real point of view.
   *How:* One short paragraph: a specific situation → what it taught you → how you work now.

3. **Quantified proof bullets**
   *Why:* Numbers = trust. "Improved performance" is air; "cut load time 40%" is evidence.
   *How:* Every win gets a metric — %, $, time saved, scale, users.

4. **Name names (brand social proof)**
   *Why:* Recognizable companies/clients borrow their credibility for you.
   *How:* "As a [role] at [Company], I…" Lead the bullet with the brand when it's known.

5. **Two-clause bullets: action → result**
   *Why:* A stat alone is trivia. Cause-and-effect shows impact.
   *How:* `[what you built/did] ; this drove [the result]`.

6. **Positioning — who you're for**
   *Why:* Specificity attracts the right fit and filters the wrong one.
   *How (job-seeker caveat):* Say who you *thrive* with — don't harshly list who you reject. A recruiter skimming shouldn't feel pushed away. Keep it warm.

7. **Short lines + whitespace**
   *Why:* 60%+ of people read LinkedIn on mobile. Dense paragraphs get skipped.
   *How:* One idea per line. Break often. Let it breathe.

8. **Keyword sprinkling + an optional Toolbox line**
   *Why:* About is searchable. Skills mentioned naturally (and a tidy tools list) help you surface.
   *How:* Weave keywords into the story; optionally end with `Toolbox: A · B · C`.

9. **One clear CTA + contact**
   *Why:* One ask gets more action than a vague "feel free to connect."
   *How:* "Building something in [your space]? Let's talk: [email]."

### Worked example (Frame B, persona: **Maya**)

```
Most product designs look beautiful in a portfolio.

Then real users touch them — and bounce.

I'm Maya. I design products people actually finish using.

Early on, I shipped a "gorgeous" checkout flow that tanked conversion. Watching ten
real users rage-quit it taught me the lesson I design by now: pretty isn't the job.
Clarity is.

What that looks like in practice:
• Redesigned a fintech onboarding flow ; this lifted activation 32% in one quarter.
• Built a design system across 4 product teams ; this cut design-to-ship time in half.
• Ran 50+ usability tests on a checkout funnel ; this recovered 18% of dropped carts.

I do my best work with teams who treat design as a lever for growth, not decoration.

Designing something your users keep abandoning? Let's fix it: maya@example.com

Toolbox: Figma · Design Systems · Usability Testing · Prototyping · Accessibility · UX Research
```

Every number here is illustrative — yours must be real.

## Honesty guardrails (don't skip)

- **Label projects vs jobs.** A side project is "(Project)" — never imply it was paid work.
- **Don't inflate years or scope.** "3+ years" counting two internships is a stretch a sharp recruiter will catch. Be defensible.
- **Simulated ≠ real.** If a result came from a demo or test, say so ("simulated"). Credibility once lost is gone.
- **You'll be asked about every line in the interview.** Write only what you can defend out loud.

## Prompt: generate Frame A (Template)

```
Write my LinkedIn About using this structure:
1) Intro: [action verb] + my role + experience + who I help and the value, with proof.
2) Three bullet wins, each with a real number.
3) Close: the specific role I want + my contact.

First person, short lines, under 2,000 characters. Skimmable.
Weave in these keywords naturally so I'm searchable: [list your keywords].

Here's my material: [paste resume + 3 wins with metrics + the role I'm targeting].
```

## Prompt: generate Frame B (Conversion copy)

```
Rewrite my LinkedIn About as conversion copy, not a résumé. Structure:
1) a hook naming my reader's #1 frustration,
2) a turning-point moment that earned my point of view,
3) the outcomes I deliver, with specific numbers,
4) who I do my best work with (warm, not exclusionary — I'm job-seeking),
5) one clear CTA + contact.

First person, short lines, under 2,000 characters. Sprinkle these keywords for search: [list].
End with a one-line Toolbox of my tools.

Flag anything that sounds inflated so I can keep it honest.

Context: [paste current About + 3 wins with metrics + who I want to work with].
```

# Part 3 — The Experience Section

Your resume bullets do **not** belong here copy-pasted. LinkedIn gives you more room and a
warmer voice — use both. Experience is also indexed for search and feeds the per-role
skills that recruiters filter on.

## The first-person myth (settled)

- **About:** first person, always.
- **Experience bullets:** the "must be first person" advice is half-right. Three styles:
  - *Third-person resume voice* ("Engineered pipelines…") — reads like a copy-paste, feels lazy.
  - *Full first person* ("I engineered…") — warm, but "I" repeated across 6 bullets gets old and wastes space.
  - *Implied first person* (drop the "I", lead with the verb, slightly conversational) — **the recommended default.**

Best practice: **role summary line in light first person, bullets in implied first person.**

Same bullet, three ways:
- Resume: `Engineered a data pipeline processing millions of records daily.`
- Full 1st person: `I engineered a data pipeline that processes millions of records daily.`
- **Implied 1st (use this):** `Built a data pipeline that moves millions of records a day — powering analytics for 12 teams.`

## The patterns that make Experience entries work

Each pattern: **what it is → why it works → how to apply.**

1. **Add a 1–2 line role summary above the bullets**
   *Why:* LinkedIn lets you write a paragraph per role; a resume doesn't. It's the #1 thing people skip.
   *How:* One human line on the scope/mission of the role before the bullets.

2. **Lead with impact, not tasks**
   *Why:* "Responsible for X" is a job description; "Cut X 40%" is a result.
   *How:* Start each bullet with a strong verb + the outcome.

3. **Keep the metrics**
   *Why:* Numbers convert here too — casual tone doesn't mean vague.
   *How:* Every bullet earns a number where one honestly exists.

4. **3–6 bullets per role, best first**
   *Why:* Mobile cuts off early and nobody reads ten bullets.
   *How:* Put your strongest 2–3 up top; trim the rest.

5. **Attach Skills to each role**
   *Why:* LinkedIn's per-role skill tags feed the recruiter-filter algorithm directly.
   *How:* Add 8–12 relevant skills to every Experience entry.

6. **Add Media / links**
   *Why:* Richer than a resume — a demo, repo, dashboard, or article builds proof.
   *How:* Attach links to roles where you can (use mock data if the real thing is private).

7. **Spell out acronyms once**
   *Why:* Helps both human readers and keyword search.
   *How:* "RAG (retrieval-augmented generation)" the first time, abbreviate after.

8. **Mirror the words in target job descriptions**
   *Why:* Experience is indexed; matching JD language surfaces you for those roles.
   *How:* Pull the recurring keywords from jobs you want; weave the true ones in.

9. **Fill every field (dates, location, title)**
   *Why:* LinkedIn ranks complete profiles higher; blank fields hurt your completeness score.
   *How:* No empty fields. Consistent date/location format across all roles.

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Write a role summary line for every position.
- Lead with outcomes and keep your numbers.
- Tag skills + add media per role.
- Spell out acronyms once.

**Don't**
- Paste resume bullets verbatim in stiff third person.
- Dump 8+ bullets — the tail goes unread.
- Leave fields blank or skip skill tags.
- Strip metrics to sound "casual."

## Worked example (persona: **Maya**, Product Designer)

**Role summary:**
```
I led design for a payments product used by small businesses — owning the path from user research to shipped, accessible flows.
```

**Bullets (implied first person):**
```
• Redesigned the onboarding flow after watching real users drop off on day one — lifting activation 32% in a quarter.

• Built a design system across 4 product teams, cutting design-to-ship time in half and ending one-off inconsistencies.

• Ran 50+ usability tests on the checkout funnel, recovering 18% of previously abandoned carts.
```

**Skill tags:** Figma · Design Systems · Usability Testing · Accessibility · UX Research · Prototyping

**Media:** case-study link or a before/after screenshot (mock data if the real work is private).

## Prompt: generate an Experience entry

```
Turn this role into a LinkedIn Experience entry (not a resume copy-paste):
- a 1-2 line role summary in light first person (the scope/mission)
- 3-6 bullets in IMPLIED first person (drop "I", lead with a strong verb)
- lead each bullet with impact, keep every real metric
- spell out acronyms once
- suggest 8-12 skills to tag on this role
- suggest 1-2 media/links I could attach (mock data if the real work is private)

Mirror the language in this target job description so I'm searchable: [paste JD or keywords].
Don't invent anything — only reframe what I give you.

Here's the role: [paste your resume bullets for this job].
```

# Part 4 — The Featured Section

Featured is your highlight reel — the cards right under About, the first thing a curious
visitor clicks. It's where you *show* proof instead of describing it. Curate ruthlessly.

## What to feature (in priority order)

1. **Your portfolio / personal website** — top slot. Shows craft and initiative in one click.
2. **Standout credentials** — published work, awards, certifications with a public page.
3. **Your best project** — the one with the strongest keywords for your target role; link the repo or demo.
4. **Your code/work profile** — GitHub, Dribbble, Behance, a publication list.
5. **A high-performing post or article** — doubles as social proof and engagement.

## The patterns that make Featured work

Each pattern: **what it is → why it works → how to apply.**

1. **Lead with your single best asset**
   *Why:* The first card gets the most clicks; mobile shows it first.
   *How:* Put the portfolio or your strongest proof in slot one.

2. **3–6 items, curated**
   *Why:* A wall of links dilutes the strong ones; nobody clicks ten cards.
   *How:* Keep only what genuinely impresses. Cut the rest.

3. **Write your own title + description — don't trust the auto-scrape**
   *Why:* LinkedIn pulls a generic preview; a keyword-rich caption with a hook converts far better.
   *How:* Override every card's title and description manually.

4. **Make the thumbnail clean**
   *Why:* Featured renders as visual cards; an ugly or blank preview kills the click.
   *How:* Set Open Graph (OG) meta tags on your site so the link preview shows a real image; for repos/files, upload a screenshot or diagram.

5. **Mix media types**
   *Why:* Link + repo + post reads as a fuller, more active profile than five identical links.
   *How:* Vary the formats across your cards.

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Lead with your portfolio or strongest proof.
- Caption every card yourself with keywords + a hook.
- Use clean thumbnails / screenshots.

**Don't**
- Dump your resume PDF here (Featured is for showing, not document storage).
- Feature half-finished work with empty READMEs — weak proof hurts more than none.
- Exceed ~6 items.

## Worked example (persona: **Maya**, Product Designer)

> **Card 1 — Portfolio (link):**
> *Title:* My Portfolio — Product Design & UX case studies
> *Description:* Hand-built site with deep dives on how I took 3 products from messy to measurable. Live prototypes + process inside.

> **Card 2 — Case study (link):**
> *Title:* Case Study — Redesigning checkout, +18% recovered carts
> *Description:* Research → prototype → test → ship. The full story behind one of my favorite wins.

> **Card 3 — Dribbble (link):**
> *Title:* Selected UI work
> *Description:* Visual highlights — design systems, mobile flows, and interaction details.

## Prompt: write your Featured captions

```
Write LinkedIn Featured card captions for these items: [list each item + what it is].
For each, give:
- a keyword-rich title (recruiters search these words)
- a 1-2 sentence description with a hook that earns the click
Keep it specific and honest. My target role is [role].
```

# Part 5 — Profile Photo & Banner

Your first visual impression. The photo builds trust; the banner is the most-wasted space
on LinkedIn — a custom one makes your profile unique and instantly *yours*.

## Profile photo

- Face fills ~60% of the frame — head and shoulders, not a distant shot.
- Look at the camera, slight smile. Profiles with faces get far more views.
- Plain, uncluttered background. Good, even lighting.
- Recent, looks like you, dressed one notch above the role's daily wear.
- High resolution, square, min 400x400px.
- No sunglasses, hats, logos, or heavy filters.

## Banner

A custom banner makes your profile stand out and should represent *you* — your work,
your brand, your stack. The default blue says nothing.

- Size: **1584 x 396 px** (4:1).
- Keep key content centered — your photo and headline cover the bottom-left, and mobile crops the sides.
- Put one of: a short tagline, your tech stack, or your portfolio URL (or a clean combination).
- Keep it simple — negative space, readable text, high contrast.
- Match the colors/font to your portfolio for a consistent personal brand.
- Avoid stock clichés (handshakes, generic skylines) and tiny text.
- Easiest path: Canva has free LinkedIn-banner templates (1584x396) — customize one in minutes.

# The meta-prompt (the real unlock)

The single most useful move: don't just ask for copy — ask the AI to **reverse-engineer
patterns** from any good example you find, explain *why* they work, and apply them.

```
Here is my current draft: [paste].
Here is an example I admire: [paste a headline / About / post you like].

Decode the example: what patterns make it work, and why does each one have impact?
Then tell me which of those patterns my draft is missing, and rewrite my draft to add
them — without inventing facts. Keep it honest and in my voice.
```

This turns any profile you admire into a reusable template — in your own words, with your
own facts.
